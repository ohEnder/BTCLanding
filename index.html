01 ‚Äî The Basics

So what is Bitcoin, exactly?

Bitcoin is digital money that no single person or institution controls. It runs on a global network of computers that anyone can join, and it's secured by math rather than trust in a bank or government.

Think of it like email for value. Just as email let you send a message to anyone on earth without a middleman, Bitcoin lets you send money to anyone, anywhere, instantly ‚Äî and no one can stop, freeze, or reverse that transaction.

It was invented in 2008 by an anonymous person (or group) named Satoshi Nakamoto, who published a 9-page paper called the Bitcoin Whitepaper. The first block was mined on January 3, 2009. Satoshi disappeared in 2011 and no one has heard from them since. Bitcoin ran itself.

The most important thing to understand: there will only ever be 21 million Bitcoin. That scarcity is hard-coded into the software and cannot be changed.

‚õì Blockchain

A public ledger of every Bitcoin transaction ever made, copied across thousands of computers worldwide. Like Google Docs ‚Äî but no one can edit old entries.

‚õè Mining

Computers compete to validate transactions by solving complex math problems. The winner adds a new "block" to the chain and earns freshly created Bitcoin as a reward.

üîë Wallets & Keys

Your Bitcoin isn't stored anywhere ‚Äî it's always on the blockchain. A "wallet" just holds your private key: a secret code proving you're allowed to spend those coins.

üìâ Decentralized

No headquarters. No CEO. No single server. If any country banned Bitcoin, the rest of the network would keep running. It's never had a single point of failure.

03 ‚Äî The Case For It

Why do people care?

üè¶

Bank the Unbanked

1.4 billion people have no access to traditional banking. Bitcoin only requires a phone and internet. No ID, no credit history, no approval needed.

üîí

Self-Custody

With Bitcoin, you are your own bank. Unlike stocks or dollars in a bank account, no one can freeze, seize, or restrict your Bitcoin without your private key.

üìä

Inflation Hedge

The US dollar has lost over 97% of its purchasing power since 1913. Bitcoin's supply is fixed. Historically, it has outperformed every major asset over long timeframes.

üåç

Borderless Transfers

Sending $10,000 overseas via a bank can take days and cost $50+. Bitcoin settles in minutes for cents. Remittances to developing nations are a major use case.

ü§ñ

Programmable Money

Bitcoin can be programmed with conditions ‚Äî like a will that automatically executes, or funds that only release when two parties agree. No lawyers required.

‚ö°

Lightning Network

A layer built on top of Bitcoin enables near-instant, near-free micro-payments. Pay for a coffee in El Salvador or tip a creator in Nigeria ‚Äî in seconds.

Think of it like digital gold

Gold has been valuable for 5,000 years because it's scarce and no government can print more of it. Bitcoin is the same idea ‚Äî but digital, portable, and with a supply cap that's mathematically enforced. You can't fake it, duplicate it, or inflate it away.

üêñ

It can actually replace your savings account

A savings account today pays 0.5‚Äì5% interest ‚Äî but the dollar in it loses purchasing power every year due to inflation. Bitcoin has outperformed cash savings over every 4-year window in its history. It's also liquid: you can sell and have cash in your bank account within 1‚Äì2 business days, just like a stock. The tradeoff is short-term volatility ‚Äî it's bumpy, but the long-term direction has always been up.

Most people treat it like a small slice

Many financial advisors suggest that if you're curious, a small allocation (1‚Äì5% of savings) gives meaningful exposure without betting the farm. Even a small amount purchased years ago turned into significant gains for early holders.

05 ‚Äî The Halving

Every 4 years, the supply cuts in half

When Bitcoin launched, miners earned 50 BTC per block. Every ~210,000 blocks (~4 years), that reward halves. This is how Bitcoin enforces scarcity over time ‚Äî and historically, halvings have preceded bull markets.

2009
Genesis

Bitcoin Launches

Block reward: 50 BTC. Price: $0. Satoshi mines the first block with the message "Chancellor on brink of second bailout for banks."

2012
1st Halving

Reward drops to 25 BTC

Price before: ~$12. One year later: ~$1,000. Bitcoin gained mainstream attention for the first time.

2016
2nd Halving

Reward drops to 12.5 BTC

Price before: ~$650. By the end of 2017: ~$20,000. The first truly global bull run ‚Äî Bitcoin appeared on mainstream news worldwide.

2020
3rd Halving

Reward drops to 6.25 BTC

Price before: ~$8,500. By Nov 2021: ~$69,000. Institutional investors ‚Äî MicroStrategy, Tesla, Square ‚Äî entered for the first time.

2024
4th Halving

Reward drops to 3.125 BTC

The first Bitcoin Spot ETF launched in the US (January 2024), followed by the halving in April. Institutional demand surged through regulated financial products.

~2028
5th Halving

Reward drops to 1.5625 BTC

As the block reward shrinks, transaction fees become increasingly important for securing the network long-term.

07 ‚Äî Common Questions

Things people always ask

Is Bitcoin just used for crime? +

Cash is used for far more crime than Bitcoin ‚Äî and Bitcoin is actually worse for criminals because every transaction is permanently public. The US government seized over $3.6 billion in Bitcoin from the Bitfinex hackers. Cash leaves no trail; Bitcoin does.

What if someone hacks Bitcoin? +

The Bitcoin network itself has never been hacked in 15+ years. It would require controlling more than 50% of all global mining power simultaneously ‚Äî an astronomically expensive and practically impossible feat. Exchange hacks (like FTX) are different ‚Äî that's like blaming Visa when a bank gets robbed.

Do I have to buy a whole Bitcoin? +

No. Bitcoin is divisible down to 8 decimal places. The smallest unit is called a "Satoshi" (1 BTC = 100,000,000 satoshis). You can buy $10 worth if you want.

Is it too late to buy Bitcoin? +

Only ~94% of all Bitcoin has been mined, and the remaining ~1.2M coins will take over 100 years to enter circulation. Whether it's "too late" depends entirely on your timeframe and goals ‚Äî but that's a question for a financial advisor, not this website.

What's the difference between Bitcoin and crypto? +

Bitcoin is the original and most decentralized cryptocurrency. "Crypto" now refers to thousands of other projects ‚Äî many of which have centralized control, pre-mined tokens, or venture capital backing. Bitcoin is the only one with no founder who can change the rules, no company behind it, and a fully fixed supply.

What happens when all 21 million are mined? +

Miners will be compensated entirely through transaction fees instead of block rewards. This won't happen until around the year 2140, so there's time to figure it out. The fee market already generates significant revenue for miners today.

08 ‚Äî Glossary

Words you'll keep seeing

Satoshi
The smallest unit of Bitcoin ‚Äî 0.00000001 BTC. Named after the creator.
Block
A batch of verified transactions added to the blockchain roughly every 10 minutes.
Private Key
A secret number that proves Bitcoin ownership. Never share this ‚Äî it IS your Bitcoin.
Seed Phrase
12 or 24 random words that back up your wallet. Store offline, never digitally.
Cold Wallet
Hardware device (like a USB) that stores Bitcoin offline. Most secure method.
Hot Wallet
Software wallet connected to the internet. Convenient but less secure than cold storage.
DCA
Dollar-Cost Averaging ‚Äî buying a fixed amount regularly (e.g., $50/week) to reduce timing risk.
HODL
Slang for holding Bitcoin long-term instead of trading. Originated from a 2013 typo on a forum.
Hash Rate
The total computing power of the Bitcoin network. Higher = more secure.
Lightning Network
A layer built on Bitcoin enabling instant, nearly free micropayments between parties.
